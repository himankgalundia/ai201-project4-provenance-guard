# Provenance Guard — Planning & Specification

A content-attribution service. A creator submits a piece of text; the system
estimates whether it was **written by a human** or **generated by AI**, returns a
**calibrated confidence score** and a **plain-language transparency label**, and
records every decision in an **audit log**. Creators can **appeal** a classification.

---

## 1. Architecture narrative (Milestone 1)

A single piece of text flows through the system like this:

1. **Client → `POST /submit`** with `{ text, creator_id }`. The request first
   passes the **rate limiter** (Flask-Limiter), which rejects abusive callers
   before any expensive work happens.
2. The route assigns a unique **`content_id`** (UUID) and persists the raw record
   in the **SQLite store** with status `classified`.
3. **Signal 1 — LLM classifier (Groq).** The text is sent to
   `llama-3.3-70b-versatile` with a prompt asking it to judge AI-vs-human and
   return a structured `ai_probability` in `[0,1]` plus a one-line rationale.
   This captures *holistic semantic / stylistic coherence*.
4. **Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python).** The same text is run
   through statistical measures (sentence-length burstiness, AI-cliché phrase
   density, lexical diversity, informality markers) to produce an independent
   `stylometric_score` in `[0,1]`. This captures *measurable structural form*.
5. **Confidence scoring.** The two signal scores are combined by a weighted
   blend into a single `ai_score`. From that we derive the **attribution**
   (`likely_ai` / `uncertain` / `likely_human`) and a **confidence** value.
6. **Transparency label.** The attribution + confidence are mapped to one of
   three plain-language label variants shown to the reader.
7. **Audit log.** A structured `classification` entry (both signal scores,
   combined score, attribution, confidence, status) is written to SQLite.
8. **Response → client.** JSON containing `content_id`, `attribution`,
   `confidence`, the individual signal scores, and the `label`.

**Appeal flow:** **Client → `POST /appeal`** with `{ content_id,
creator_reasoning }` → the content record's status is updated to
`under_review` and the reasoning is stored → an `appeal` entry is appended to
the audit log alongside the original decision → a confirmation is returned. No
automated re-classification; the item now waits in a human review queue.

## 2. Detection signals (Question 1)

We use **two genuinely independent signals**: one *semantic* (what the text
means / how coherent it reads) and one *structural* (measurable statistics of
the form). They can disagree, and that disagreement is itself information.

### Signal 1 — LLM classifier (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)
- **Measures:** holistic "does this read as machine-generated?" — semantic
  coherence, tone, the subtle over-smoothness LLMs tend to produce.
- **Why it differs human vs AI:** a large model has seen vast amounts of both
  human and AI text and is sensitive to high-level patterns no single statistic
  captures (e.g. generic hedging, even informational density, lack of lived
  specificity).
- **Output:** JSON `{ ai_probability: float in [0,1], reasoning: str }`.
  `1.0` = definitely AI, `0.0` = definitely human.
- **Blind spot:** it is itself an LLM — it can be confidently wrong, is
  non-deterministic, can be fooled by AI text that was lightly human-edited, and
  may penalize *formal* human writing (academic/legal) as "AI-like." It is also
  a network dependency that can fail or rate-limit. (When it fails we fall back
  to a neutral `0.5` and flag the result as degraded.)

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python)
Four sub-metrics, each turned into an AI-likeness contribution in `[0,1]`, then
blended into one `stylometric_score`:
- **Burstiness — sentence-length coefficient of variation (weight 0.40).** Human
  writing mixes short punchy and long winding sentences (high variance); AI text
  is more uniform (low variance). Low CV ⇒ AI-like.
- **AI-cliché / transition density (weight 0.30).** Frequency of formulaic
  connectives ("furthermore", "moreover", "it is important to note", "in
  conclusion", "delve", "tapestry", …). High density ⇒ AI-like.
- **Informality markers (weight 0.20).** Contractions, lowercase sentence
  starts, casual interjections ("ok so", "honestly", "lol"). More ⇒ human-like
  (pushes score down).
- **Lexical diversity — type-token ratio (weight 0.10).** Very repetitive
  connective vocabulary nudges AI-like; included with low weight because TTR is
  noisy on short texts.
- **Output:** `{ stylometric_score: float in [0,1], metrics: {...} }`.
- **Blind spot:** statistics only — no understanding of meaning. A human poem
  with deliberate repetition and simple words can look "uniform" and score
  AI-like; a casually-prompted AI told to "write informally with typos" can
  spoof the informality markers. Short inputs (< ~3 sentences) give unstable
  variance, so we down-weight confidence there.

### Combining the signals (Question 1 cont.)
```
ai_score = 0.65 * llm_ai_probability + 0.35 * stylometric_score
```
The LLM gets the larger weight because it is the more holistic, generally more
accurate signal; the stylometric signal acts as an independent, transparent,
always-available cross-check that can pull a borderline LLM call back toward
"uncertain." If the LLM signal is in degraded (fallback) mode, weights shift to
`0.20 / 0.80` so a failed network call cannot masquerade as a real semantic
verdict.

## 3. Uncertainty representation (Question 2)

`ai_score ∈ [0,1]` is the system's estimated probability that the text is AI.
The **attribution** is decided by two thresholds:

| `ai_score` range      | attribution    |
|-----------------------|----------------|
| `>= 0.65`             | `likely_ai`    |
| `0.35 < score < 0.65` | `uncertain`    |
| `<= 0.35`             | `likely_human` |

The reported **`confidence` = `max(ai_score, 1 - ai_score)`**, i.e. the
probability of whichever class the system picked. It ranges **0.50 → 1.00**:

- `ai_score = 0.95` → likely_ai, **confidence 0.95** (strong).
- `ai_score = 0.51` → uncertain, **confidence 0.51** (barely leaning — genuinely
  unsure).
- `ai_score = 0.05` → likely_human, **confidence 0.95**.

This is deliberately *not* a binary flip at 0.5: the middle band (0.35–0.65) is a
real "uncertain" zone, and the confidence number degrades smoothly toward 0.50
as the two signals weaken or disagree. **"What does 0.6 mean?"** → the system
thinks there's a 60% chance the text is AI; that lands in the uncertain band, so
the reader is told the origin is unclear rather than being given a false verdict.

Calibration is checked empirically in `test_scoring.py` against four labeled
inputs spanning the range (clear AI, clear human, formal human, lightly-edited
AI); see README for the observed scores.

## 4. Transparency label design (Question 3)

Three variants, chosen by attribution. Each shows a plain headline, a
plain-language body, and the confidence as a percentage so a non-technical reader
can gauge how sure the system is. Every variant states it is an *automated
estimate* and points to the appeal path.

**Variant A — High-confidence AI** (`attribution = likely_ai`):
> 🤖 **Likely AI-Generated** — Our analysis suggests this content was most likely
> produced by an AI system (confidence: {pct}%). Both our language-model check and
> our writing-style check pointed toward automated generation. This is an
> automated estimate, not a certainty. If you wrote this yourself, you can appeal.

**Variant B — High-confidence human** (`attribution = likely_human`):
> ✍️ **Likely Human-Written** — Our analysis suggests this content was most likely
> written by a person (confidence: {pct}%). The writing showed the natural
> variation and voice typical of human authors. This is an automated estimate and
> may be wrong.

**Variant C — Uncertain** (`attribution = uncertain`):
> ❓ **Attribution Uncertain** — We could not confidently determine whether this
> content was written by a person or generated by AI (confidence: {pct}%). Our two
> checks were borderline or disagreed with each other. Please treat the origin of
> this content as unknown.

## 5. Appeals workflow (Question 4)

- **Who:** the creator of the content (anyone holding the `content_id`; in a real
  system this would be gated by auth tying `creator_id` to an account).
- **What they provide:** the `content_id` and free-text `creator_reasoning`
  explaining why they believe the classification is wrong.
- **What the system does:** validates the `content_id` exists → sets the content
  record's `status` to **`under_review`** and stores the reasoning → appends an
  **`appeal`** entry to the audit log that references the original decision (so the
  reviewer sees both side by side) → returns a confirmation `{ status:
  "under_review", message, appeal_logged: true }`.
- **What a human reviewer sees in the queue:** `GET /log` (or a filtered queue
  view) surfaces every item with `status = under_review`, each showing the
  original attribution + confidence + both signal scores **and** the creator's
  reasoning. No automated re-classification — a person makes the final call.

## 6. Anticipated edge cases (Question 5)

1. **Repetitive, simple-vocabulary poetry.** A human poem built on refrains and
   short, plain lines produces *low sentence-length variance* and *low lexical
   diversity* — exactly the structural fingerprint of AI text. The stylometric
   signal will likely over-score it as AI. Mitigation: the LLM signal (which
   understands it's a poem) should pull it back, and the 0.35–0.65 band means a
   split decision lands in "uncertain" rather than a false "AI" verdict.
2. **Formal human writing (academic / legal / financial prose).** Long, uniform,
   cliché-heavy sentences ("the relationship between … has been extensively
   studied in the literature") trip *both* signals toward AI. This is our most
   likely false positive. The confidence band and the appeal workflow exist
   precisely for this case — a non-native or formal writer can contest it.
3. **Very short inputs (one or two sentences).** Burstiness and TTR are unstable
   with too little text; the system should land near "uncertain" rather than
   over-commit. (We require a minimum text length and down-weight short inputs.)
4. **Lightly human-edited AI output.** Genuinely hard — the structure is smoothed
   by the model but the obvious tells are edited out. We *want* these to land
   mid-range (uncertain), not falsely "human."

### False-positive trace (Milestone 1 requirement)
A human academic submits formal prose. Both signals lean AI → `ai_score ≈ 0.7`
→ attribution `likely_ai`, confidence ≈ 0.70. The reader sees Variant A, but it
is phrased as an *estimate* and explicitly invites appeal. The creator submits
`POST /appeal` with reasoning ("I wrote this; I'm a non-native speaker and write
formally"). Status flips to `under_review`, the appeal is logged beside the
original decision, and a human reviewer resolves it. The confidence (0.70, not
0.95) already signaled this was not a slam-dunk call.

## Architecture (diagram + narrative)

**Submission flow**
```
                         [rate limiter: 10/min, 100/day]
client --(text, creator_id)--> POST /submit
                                   |
                                   v
                         assign content_id (UUID)
                         store raw record (status=classified)
                                   |
                 +-----------------+------------------+
                 v                                    v
   Signal 1: Groq LLM                    Signal 2: stylometric heuristics
   --(text)-->  llm_ai_probability        --(text)-->  stylometric_score
                 [0..1]                                  [0..1]
                 |                                    |
                 +-----------------+------------------+
                                   v
              ai_score = 0.65*llm + 0.35*stylometric   --(combined score)-->
                                   |
                                   v
        attribution + confidence  (thresholds: .35 / .65)
                                   |
                                   v
                 transparency label (Variant A / B / C)  --(label text)-->
                                   |
                                   v
        audit log entry  {content_id, scores, attribution, confidence, status}
                                   |
                                   v
   response: {content_id, attribution, confidence, llm_score,
              stylometric_score, ai_score, label, status}
```

**Appeal flow**
```
client --(content_id, creator_reasoning)--> POST /appeal
                                   |
                                   v
                  look up content record by content_id
                                   |
                                   v
        status: classified --> under_review ; store creator_reasoning
                                   |
                                   v
   audit log entry  {event: appeal, content_id, creator_reasoning,
                     original_attribution, original_confidence}
                                   |
                                   v
        response: {status: under_review, appeal_logged: true, message}
```

*Narrative:* The submission flow turns raw text into a labeled, logged decision
by fanning out to two independent signals, blending them into one calibrated
score, and mapping that score to a reader-facing label. The appeal flow lets a
creator contest a decision: it never re-runs detection, it simply flips the
item's status to `under_review` and records the creator's reasoning next to the
original verdict for a human to judge.

## Stretch features (update before starting)

- **Ensemble detection (3+ signals):** the stylometric signal is already an
  *internal ensemble* of four weighted sub-metrics (burstiness, cliché density,
  informality, lexical diversity) combined with the LLM signal — documented
  weighting is in §2. A future third top-level signal (e.g. a perplexity proxy)
  could be added to the same weighted blend.
